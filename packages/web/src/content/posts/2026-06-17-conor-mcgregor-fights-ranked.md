Conor McGregor has headlined some of the largest events in combat sports history and, along the way, been part of some of its most entertaining fights. With his return against Max Holloway at [UFC 329 on July 11](/blog/mcgregor-holloway-2-ufc-329), we went back through his entire career and ranked it.

The ranking is not ours, exactly. Good Fights users rate every bout on a ten-point scale, and this list orders McGregor's fights by their average rating, highest to lowest. That means it measures something specific: not who won, not how famous the night was, but how good the fight was to watch. A close, five-round war can outrank a 13-second knockout, and a loss can outrank a routine win. Two notes before the list. Ratings reflect how many people scored each fight, so the bouts near the bottom are not necessarily bad, only less universally loved, and a couple of the older fights have far fewer ratings than the headline events. And you do not need to know McGregor's catalogue to follow along: each entry explains who he was fighting and what was at stake.

## 1. Conor McGregor vs Nate Diaz 2 (2016)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="f16940ca-39c6-4f7a-8050-58e4bb05293b" data-rank="1"></div>

McGregor's highest-rated fight is a rematch he spent five months obsessing over. At UFC 196 earlier that year, Nate Diaz, a durable, high-volume boxer from Stockton, California, had handed McGregor the first loss of his UFC career. McGregor demanded an immediate do-over and insisted it stay at welterweight, 170 pounds, so neither man had to cut much weight.

What made the rematch compelling was the adjustment. In every previous fight McGregor had hunted an early knockout. Here he did the opposite. He attacked Diaz's lead leg with kicks to slow him down, picked his moments rather than loading up, and scored knockdowns across the first two rounds, badly marking up Diaz's face. Then the fight turned. Diaz found his rhythm in the third, pressed McGregor against the fence, and piled on volume while a visibly exhausted McGregor backed away for long stretches. McGregor recovered enough in the fourth to circle and land clean left hands, and the fifth was a grind, with Diaz pushing forward and securing a late takedown as the final bell sounded. The judges scored it a majority decision for McGregor, 48-47, 47-47, 48-47.

It was the first evidence that McGregor could plan and survive a long, difficult fight rather than simply end an early one, and the two men finished it with obvious mutual respect. "The king is back," he said afterward.

## 2. Conor McGregor vs Nate Diaz 1 (2016)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="d6877d85-7939-458b-913c-a40f5c1feae4" data-rank="2"></div>

This is the fight that first made McGregor look beatable. He had been booked to challenge for the lightweight title, but the champion withdrew injured, and Diaz stepped in on short notice at welterweight. It was a significant jump in size against a notoriously tough opponent.

The buildup was its own story. For the first time, McGregor's pre-fight needling did not seem to land. Diaz was unbothered and fired the insults straight back, and the fight followed the same pattern. McGregor started fast and connected with the left hand that had dropped everyone before him, but Diaz took it and kept walking forward. As McGregor tired, Diaz's boxing took over. McGregor, fatigued, shot for a takedown, something he rarely needed to do, and Diaz used the scramble to take his back and finish him with a rear-naked choke in the second round.

McGregor's response was telling. Rather than make excuses, he leaned on a phrase from his longtime coach, John Kavanagh, who later titled a book with it: you win or you learn. He admitted he had underestimated what the extra weight would do to Diaz's resilience, noting that a smaller man would not have absorbed those shots. He took the lesson into the rematch.

## 3. Conor McGregor vs Dustin Poirier 2 (2021)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="4d177d71-e10b-4dc6-8641-5af9d9c00fe0" data-rank="3"></div>

Seven years after McGregor knocked Poirier out, the rematch was a different fight against a very different opponent. Poirier had become one of the most complete lightweights in the world, and he arrived with a specific plan.

The opening round was competitive, with McGregor landing shoulder strikes in the clinch and finding some success. Then Poirier began chopping at McGregor's lead leg with low calf kicks, a tactic that quietly takes away a striker's mobility and power. By the second round McGregor's lead leg was compromised, his footwork was gone, and Poirier opened up and stopped him with punches. It was the first time McGregor had been finished by strikes in MMA.

The result flipped a rivalry that had looked settled and exposed a real vulnerability, one that McGregor's long stretches of inactivity, only a single fight in the previous fourteen months, did nothing to help.

## 4. Conor McGregor vs Khabib Nurmagomedov (2018)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="0a1ba0bd-f3c9-41b5-8a76-5c9da32d26fb" data-rank="4"></div>

This was the biggest event of McGregor's career and the most personal rivalry the sport has produced. The buildup turned genuinely ugly, including an incident months earlier when McGregor threw a hand truck at a bus carrying Khabib and other fighters. The pay-per-view numbers were enormous.

The fight was a one-sided demonstration of what Khabib does to everyone. He weathered the early going, took McGregor down, and battered him with ground-and-pound. The constant threat of the takedown was almost as damaging as the takedown itself: it forced McGregor to drop his hands and absorb clean punches he would normally have avoided. Khabib finished it with a neck crank in the fourth round, then leapt the cage to go after a member of McGregor's team, setting off a chaotic post-fight brawl.

It also marked a shift. The McGregor in this fight seemed less composed than the calm, calculating version of his rise, and a popular theory took hold that the money and fame from the Mayweather bout had dulled his hunger. The boxing trainer Teddy Atlas, among others, reached for an old Marvin Hagler line to describe him: it is hard to get up and do roadwork at five in the morning when you have been sleeping in silk pajamas. Khabib would retire without ever granting the rematch McGregor wanted.

## 5. Conor McGregor vs Eddie Alvarez (2016)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="0eaa311b-3c71-487e-87d1-2740e3477a95" data-rank="5"></div>

At UFC 205, the promotion's first event at Madison Square Garden, McGregor held the featherweight title and challenged Eddie Alvarez, the reigning lightweight champion. A win would make him the first fighter in UFC history to hold titles in two weight classes at the same time.

It was a masterclass. McGregor took the center of the cage immediately, controlled the distance, and dropped Alvarez with sharp combinations inside the first round, his speed and reach making an accomplished champion look out of his depth. When Alvarez tried to shoot for takedowns in the second, McGregor stuffed them, then landed a clean four-punch combination that put Alvarez down again and ended it. The combination became one of the signature images of his career.

For all the fights with more drama above it on this list, this was arguably McGregor's most technically flawless title performance, and it gave him a piece of history.

## 6. Conor McGregor vs Chad Mendes (2015)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="ac907fa0-9a43-4132-a7c4-ec7fd29a41ce" data-rank="6"></div>

UFC 189 was the first pay-per-view McGregor headlined, and the event around it became part of the sport's history. He had been scheduled to fight featherweight champion Jose Aldo after a multi-country promotional World Tour, the Dublin leg of which drew tens of thousands of fans. When Aldo withdrew with a rib injury, the elite wrestler Chad Mendes stepped in on short notice to fight for an interim title.

![Conor McGregor on the UFC 189 World Tour in 2015](/blog/mcgregor-ufc189-2015.jpg)
*Conor McGregor on the UFC 189 World Tour, 2015. Photo: Andrius Petrucenia / CC BY-SA 2.0 (cropped).*

The fight itself was the first time McGregor faced a high-level wrestler, and for a while it went badly for him. Mendes took him down and did real damage with ground-and-pound in the second round. But rather than hold the dominant position, Mendes went for a guillotine choke, McGregor scrambled free, and they returned to the feet. There, with a new urgency, McGregor unloaded and finished Mendes with strikes in the final seconds of the round to win the interim featherweight belt.

The night is remembered as much for its atmosphere as its fights. An estimated 10,000 or more Irish fans traveled to Las Vegas, packing the arena even for the weigh-ins, and the UFC staged rare live musical walkouts, with Sinead O'Connor performing for McGregor. With a stacked card that also featured Robbie Lawler against Rory MacDonald, UFC 189 is still cited as one of the best events the promotion has put on.

## 7. Conor McGregor vs Dustin Poirier 3 (2021)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="dcced014-d20d-4626-a596-806e6ad5fb46" data-rank="7"></div>

The trilogy decider, with the rivalry tied at one win each, is the grimmest entry on the list. The two traded in the opening round, with Poirier finding his range, and then it ended in a way no one wanted. In the closing seconds of the first round, McGregor stepped back onto his lower leg at an awkward angle and it snapped. The fight was waved off by doctor's stoppage between rounds.

There was barely a fight to evaluate, which is reflected in how it rates, but the consequences were large. The injury began the long layoff that McGregor is only now returning from at UFC 329.

## 8. Conor McGregor vs Marcus Brimage (2013)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="f2a42b6c-b111-4470-b827-6c4ff0fa51b8" data-rank="8"></div>

This is McGregor's UFC debut, and a note of caution: it has only a handful of ratings, far fewer than the major events, so its high placement reflects a small group of enthusiastic scores rather than a broad consensus.

McGregor arrived in the UFC already a draw. He had held two titles at once, featherweight and lightweight, in the London-based Cage Warriors promotion, and had built a devoted following in Ireland on the strength of his charisma and one-punch knockouts. The story goes that Dana White was so overwhelmed by people urging him to sign McGregor during a trip to Ireland that he did.

The fight was a tidy introduction to his style. He drew Marcus Brimage in, baited the exchange, and timed a counter, the pull-counter that became his trademark: invite the opponent to throw, slip just out of range, then land hard in the opening they leave behind. He finished it about a minute into the first round and took home a Knockout of the Night bonus.

## 9. Conor McGregor vs Jose Aldo (2015)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="ed79d1b9-8de4-4636-bb5d-305e88a8362c" data-rank="9"></div>

It is the most famous knockout of McGregor's career, and it is worth pausing on why it does not rank higher here. The answer is the same thing that made it iconic: it lasted 13 seconds. There was almost nothing to watch, so a fight built on drama and back-and-forth rates it below the wars, even as it stands as one of the most significant moments in the sport.

Jose Aldo had not lost in roughly a decade and was the only featherweight champion the UFC had ever crowned. After a long, heated buildup, the unification fight was over almost before it began. McGregor goaded Aldo into a hard right hand, slipped inside it, and landed a counter left as the champion came forward, dropping him and finishing immediately. It remains the fastest finish in UFC title-fight history.

Two details capture how deliberate it was. Footage later emerged of McGregor drilling that exact counter backstage before the fight, and he had said beforehand that he expected to agitate Aldo into throwing an undisciplined punch he could time. "Precision beats power, and timing beats speed," he said afterward. The plan and the result matched exactly.

## 10. Conor McGregor vs Dustin Poirier 1 (2014)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="04c424b1-569b-43c7-ab03-f9744b92abed" data-rank="10"></div>

This was the fight that proved McGregor's hype translated against top competition. Dustin Poirier was a rising featherweight contender and a real test, and McGregor passed it convincingly, using his reach and counter-striking to hurt Poirier and finish him inside the first round.

It is also where the showman fully arrived. McGregor had predicted a first-round finish, and afterward leaned into the persona that stuck: he did not just knock opponents out, he said, he picked the round, which earned him the nickname Mystic Mac. He used the moment to begin calling out Jose Aldo, planting the rivalry that would carry him to genuine stardom. Years before their trilogy turned bitter, this first meeting with Poirier was simply a breakout.

## 11. Conor McGregor vs Max Holloway 1 (2013)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="15c1663f-210f-4459-b022-f7a3546bda9d" data-rank="11"></div>

This fight looks very different now than it did at the time, which is exactly why the [UFC 329 rematch](/blog/mcgregor-holloway-2-ufc-329) is so unusual. McGregor outpointed a 21-year-old Max Holloway over three rounds, the only decision in McGregor's first 22 professional fights. The man he beat went on to become one of the greatest featherweights in history.

There is a hidden story in the result. Midway through the fight, in a grappling exchange, McGregor tore the anterior cruciate ligament in his left knee. Unable to strike effectively or stand securely on the damaged leg, he changed plans entirely, took Holloway down, and controlled him on the ground for the rest of the fight to secure the decision. The injury required surgery and kept him out for the better part of a year. That he won at all, on one leg and improvising, is more impressive in hindsight than it appeared on the night.

## 12. Conor McGregor vs Diego Brandao (2014)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="e29e4044-c0da-4eb8-9f75-723f5dc3cfa0" data-rank="12"></div>

This is the night McGregor's star truly took off. Headlining in his home city of Dublin for the first time, in front of a deafening home crowd, he showcased the same counter-boxing and stopped Diego Brandao in the first round, earning a Performance of the Night bonus. It was his second first-round finish in the UFC, and it made the wider audience pay attention.

It also produced one of his enduring lines. With Irish fighters going 4-0 on the card, McGregor told the crowd, "We're not here to take part, we're here to take over." The result was a competent finish over a tough opponent, but the moment was bigger than the fight.

## 13. Conor McGregor vs Dennis Siver (2015)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="56b8b4e2-6dbd-4891-b66e-860eedb80ab0" data-rank="13"></div>

McGregor dismantled Dennis Siver over two rounds with kicks and boxing before a second-round stoppage. The win was a formality. What is remembered is what came after: McGregor leapt over the cage and into the crowd to confront Jose Aldo, who was sitting at cageside, a piece of theater that helped sell the title fight to come.

The Siver week was also the first time McGregor talked in detail about how he prepares, describing how he studies an opponent's body type and movement patterns and trains specifically for it. He dismissed Siver as a wrestler with an overhand who gases, whose build worked against him. As interest in McGregor grew, that analytical side became a recurring theme, a counterpoint to the trash talk: he was studying these fights as carefully as he was promoting them.

## 14. Conor McGregor vs Donald Cerrone (2020)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="2ae3e73d-294d-40ca-bc6f-61db07841908" data-rank="14"></div>

Returning after a 15-month absence following the Khabib loss and the Mayweather bout, McGregor needed a clean night, and he got one against Donald Cerrone, a popular and experienced veteran. It was over almost immediately.

McGregor used an unusual tactic in the clinch, throwing shoulder strikes that bloodied and broke Cerrone's nose, then created the space for a head kick and punches that ended it 40 seconds into the first round. It was the second-fastest win of his career, behind only the Aldo knockout. The performance answered the question of whether he could still look dangerous, and it remains, to date, his most recent victory.

## 15. Conor McGregor vs Floyd Mayweather (2017)

<div class="gf-fight-card" data-fight-id="c7d58984-4e04-4dad-a953-aa9e57754a7f" data-rank="15"></div>

This one was not an MMA fight at all, but it is impossible to leave off. After becoming a two-division UFC champion, McGregor crossed over into a professional boxing match against Floyd Mayweather, one of the greatest boxers of his era. That the UFC allowed one of its champions to do this was itself remarkable. The promotion would not let another fighter take such a bout, but McGregor had leverage few athletes ever have, and Dana White helped promote it.

The fight was more competitive than skeptics expected. McGregor, who had never boxed professionally, was busy early and won some of the opening rounds on activity, landing a few shots that had the crowd believing. Then experience took over. Mayweather, pacing himself, picked McGregor apart as he tired, and the referee stopped it in the tenth round with McGregor exhausted and no longer answering punches.

As a sporting result it was a loss. As a spectacle and a business event it was one of the largest combat-sports nights of the century, and it cemented McGregor as a genuine crossover star, for better and, given the questions about his motivation afterward, arguably for worse.

## What is Conor McGregor's record?

McGregor holds a professional MMA record of 22 wins and 6 losses, with 19 of those wins by knockout or TKO. He is also 0-1 as a professional boxer, from the 2017 loss to Floyd Mayweather. His UFC career includes the featherweight title, the lightweight title, and a long stretch as the promotion's biggest pay-per-view draw.

## Why is the Aldo knockout not ranked first?

Because this list ranks fights by how Good Fights users rated them, not by fame or importance, and a 13-second knockout simply gives fans less to watch and rate highly than a five-round war. The Aldo finish is arguably McGregor's most significant moment and one of the most important in UFC history, but the wars with Nate Diaz, and his closest competitive fights, tend to rate higher because there was more fight to enjoy. Different measures produce different lists, which is part of the point.

## Has Conor McGregor ever been knocked out or finished?

Yes. He has been submitted twice, by Nate Diaz in 2016 and Khabib Nurmagomedov in 2018, stopped by strikes once, by Dustin Poirier in 2021, and lost by doctor's stoppage after breaking his leg against Poirier later that year. He was also stopped by Floyd Mayweather in their boxing match. He has not been knocked cold by a single punch in MMA, but he has been finished.

It is the strangest entry in the timeline. McGregor beat Holloway once, more than a decade ago, while tearing his ACL, and now returns from a five-year layoff to face a man who became an all-time great in the years since.
